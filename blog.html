About Me – R.C. Mwelwa


I am Robert.Chikonde. Mwelwa, the founder of ZARI Zambia African Reborn Initiative. A proud Zambian man, 39 years of age, I have dedicated my life to uncovering and restoring the African culture and traditions that have been systematically eroded by religion, the education system, and political structures. These foreign influences have played a significant role in disconnecting us from the wisdom and practices that sustained our forefathers for generations.


My Birth: A Spiritual Revelation


My very existence is deeply intertwined with ancestral intervention. When my mother was pregnant, she had no idea that she was carrying a lifeless child. It wasn’t until 12 months into the pregnancy that the ancestors visited her in a vision and told her she would conceive a baby boy. They instructed her to go to the hospital, and on August 15, 1986, at exactly 19:00 hours, I was born. Astonishingly, my mother and I share the same birth date and exact time of birth a divine confirmation of the spiritual connection I have carried since birth.


A Life Marked by Ancestral Protection


My journey has not been an easy one. Even after birth, there were attempts to take my life, yet time and time again, my ancestors have stood by me, ensuring my survival. To this day, I continue to navigate challenges, but I do so with thestrength and guidance of those who walked before me. This is just the beginning of my story.Keep an eye on this page as I reveal more about my life journey, the battles I have faced, and the mission that drives me to bring Africa back to its roots.

Mission


I created Back to My Roots as a deeply personal journey a path toward rediscovering and embracing the rich cultural and traditional heritage that has been eroded over time. My goal is to reconnect with the wisdom of my ancestors, revive the customs that once defined my identity, and take pride in who I truly am. This is about returning to my source honoring the traditions that sustained my forefathers long before foreign religions were imposed upon us.


Vision


I dream of a future where I, and others like me, can embrace our African traditions without fear or shame. A future where spirituality is not dictated by foreign doctrines but deeply rooted in indigenous beliefs. I want to break free from the false narratives that have demonized our cultural practices and, instead, pass down the beauty of our heritage to future generations.


Why This Journey Matters to Me


For centuries, African traditions have been overshadowed by foreign religions that labeled them as evil, backward, or primitive. Colonization, reinforced by religious institutions, systematically stripped us of our ancestral wisdom, replacing it with doctrines that served foreign interests. Our spiritual systems were dismissed, our gods were rejected, and our sacred rituals were cast aside.


But why? The answer is simple control. By disconnecting us from our roots, they made us vulnerable, dependent, and ashamed of our own heritage. They told us that salvation could only be found in their religion, yet my ancestors thrived spiritually, mentally, and physically long before these ideologies arrived. Now, I am on a journey to undo this damage and return to what is rightfully mine.


How Religion Has Changed My Cultural Identity


As I reflect on my personal experiences and the larger African narrative, I see how religious influence has altered our perception of ourselves and our traditions:


• Fear of Ancestors I was taught to fear my ancestors rather than honor them as guardians who guide and protect me. This disconnect left me lost, searching for answers outside of my own lineage.


• Loss of Spirituality Indigenous African spirituality, which celebrates nature, the spirit world, and our deep connection to the universe, was replaced with rigid beliefs that alienated us from our power.


• Westernization of Identity Many Africans, including myself at one point, have been encouraged to adopt foreign names, languages, and practices, believing them to be superior.


• Submission Through Faith Religion was used to pacify Africans, teaching us to accept suffering on earth for the promise of heaven while others prospered from our resources and ignorance.


Reclaiming What Was Lost


I am on a mission to reclaim what was stolen. This means:


• Learning my history Understanding the greatness of African civilizations before colonization.


• Practicing my traditions Engaging in spiritual practices, rites of passage, and cultural ceremonies.


• Speaking my language Preserving and passing down my native tongue.


• Honoring my ancestors Recognizing and seeking guidance from those who walked before me.


• Challenging false narratives Rejecting the lies that have kept me disconnected from my roots.


A Personal Journey, A Personal Choice


This journey is mine. It is about my truth, my heritage, my spiritual awakening. I choose to embrace who I truly am. I choose to go back to my roots.

        Back to My Roots: Reclaiming African Spirituality

        By R.C. Mwelwa | Last Updated: 2/20/2025

        African Spirituality

        Introduction

        For centuries, Africa’s rich cultural heritage has been eroded by religious and political influences. This blog is dedicated to reviving our ancestral wisdom and reconnecting with our true identity.

        Why African Spirituality Matters

        Before foreign religions arrived, African societies thrived through their spiritual systems. Our traditions emphasized:

        • Ancestral Worship: Honoring the spirits of those who came before us.
        • Nature’s Connection: Recognizing the spiritual power of rivers, mountains, and trees.
        • Community Rituals: Strengthening bonds through shared customs and ceremonies.

        How Religion Disconnected Us

        Religious colonization labeled our spiritual systems as witchcraft. Our gods were demonized, our names replaced, and our traditions dismissed as primitive.

        Steps to Reconnect With Your Roots

        It’s time to reclaim what was stolen. Here’s how:

        1. Learn Our History: Study African traditions before colonization.
        2. Practice Our Rituals: Engage in cultural ceremonies and spiritual practices.
        3. Speak Our Languages: Preserve and pass down native tongues.
        4. Honor Our Ancestors: Seek guidance from ancestral spirits.
